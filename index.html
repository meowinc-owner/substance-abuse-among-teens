Know What's Out There

Alcohol

MOST COMMON

The most used substance among Canadian students. It slows your brain, damages your liver, and leads to risky decisions.

22% of students used alcohol in the past 30 days

Vaping / E-Cigs

RISING FAST

Often contains nicotine, which is extremely addictive and can harm the parts of your brain that control attention and learning.

15% of students vaped in the past 30 days

Cannabis / Weed

COMMON

Regular use is linked to anxiety, memory problems, and dependency — especially when your brain is still developing.

12% of students used cannabis in the past 30 days

Prescription Drugs

MISUNDERSTOOD

Using someone else's medication (like ADHD pills or opioids) is dangerous and can lead to overdose or long-term health issues.

1 in 4 teens think prescription drugs are safer than street drugs — they're not

Fentanyl

EXTREMELY DANGEROUS

A deadly drug being hidden in fake pills. An amount the size of a grain of salt can kill. Never take a pill you didn't get from a pharmacy.

Leading cause of drug-related death in North America

Inhalants

OFTEN OVERLOOKED

Sniffing glue, aerosols, or markers can cause brain damage, hearing loss, or sudden death — even on the very first try.

Most commonly misused substance by 12-year-olds

Your brain develops until 25. Every hit matters.

Your Brain Isn't Done Yet

Your brain is still developing until you're about 25. Substances can permanently change how it grows — affecting memory, learning, and decision-making.

Addiction Happens Fast

Teen brains get addicted way faster than adult brains. What starts as "just trying it once" can turn into a habit before you even realize it.

Mental Health Takes a Hit

Regular cannabis use is linked to anxiety, depression, and memory problems in teens. Substances don't fix stress — they make it worse.

Risky Situations

Being under the influence makes you more likely to get into accidents, make unsafe choices, or be taken advantage of.

Warning
Signs

If you notice these in yourself or a friend, it might be time to talk to someone you trust.

Behavioural

  • Grades suddenly dropping
  • Sudden change in friend groups
  • Pulling away from family
  • Loss of interest in hobbies

Physical

  • Bloodshot or glazed eyes
  • Changes in sleep or appetite
  • Neglecting personal hygiene

Emotional

  • Unusual mood swings or anger
  • Increased secrecy
  • Needing money with no explanation

Remember: Noticing signs in a friend doesn't mean you're ratting them out, it means you care. Talking to a trusted adult could save their life.

Myth vs.
Reality

Tap each myth to reveal the truth. These are the most common excuses people use.

Reality

Actually, most Canadian teens DON'T use drugs or alcohol. The majority of students are substance-free. You're in good company if you say no.

Reality

Addiction changes your brain chemistry. Teen brains are especially vulnerable, what feels like a free choice now can become something you can't control.

Reality

"Natural" doesn't mean safe. Regular cannabis use is directly linked to anxiety, memory problems, and dependency in teens under 25.

Reality

Vape clouds contain nicotine, heavy metals, and chemicals that harm your lungs and brain. It's not steam, it's an aerosol full of harmful stuff.

Reality

Fake pills laced with fentanyl look identical to real ones. One pill can be lethal. You can never be sure what's actually in it.

Section 03 — How to say no

Your
Strategy
for Success

Making healthy choices today protects your brain and keeps your future goals on track.

Set SMART Goals

Focus on your future. Sports, grades, or personal projects keep you motivated and give you something to protect.

Manage Stress Differently

Instead of substances, try exercise, mindfulness, or talking to a friend. These actually work long-term.

Pick Your People

Surround yourself with friends who respect your choice to stay substance-free. The right people won't pressure you.

Ready-to-Go Responses

Practice these until they feel natural.

"Nah, I'm good."

Short, simple, no explanation needed.

"I have a big game tomorrow."

A solid excuse that's hard to argue with.

"That stuff's not for me."

Confident and clear.

"My parents would literally end me."

Blame the parents. classic and effective.

"I'll stick with water/soda."

Just redirect to something else.

"I promised [someone] I wouldn't."

Hard for anyone to push past a promise.
